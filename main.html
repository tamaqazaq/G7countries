About G7

The Group of Seven (G7) is an intergovernmental political and economic forum consisting of Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, the United Kingdom, and the United States; additionally, the European Union (EU) is a "non-enumerated member". It is organized around shared values of pluralism, liberal democracy, and representative government. G7 members are major IMF advanced economies. Originating from an ad hoc gathering of finance ministers in 1973, the G7 has since become a formal, high-profile venue for discussing and coordinating solutions to major global issues, especially in the areas of trade, security, economics, and climate change. Each member's head of government or state, along with the EU's Commission President and European Council President, meet annually at the G7 Summit; other high-ranking officials of the G7 and the EU meet throughout the year. Representatives of other states and international organizations are often invited as guests, with Russia having been a formal member (as part of the G8) from 1997 until its expulsion in 2014.

The G7 operates without a formal treaty, permanent secretariat, or official headquarters. Its leadership rotates annually among the member countries, with the presiding nation setting the group's agenda and hosting the annual summit. In 2024, Italy holds the presidency. Despite lacking a legal foundation, the G7 is recognized for its considerable global influence. The group has played a key role in driving several major international efforts, such as fighting the HIV/AIDS pandemic, providing financial support to developing nations, and addressing climate change through agreements like the Paris Accord. Nonetheless, the G7 has faced criticism for its limited membership, lack of diversity in global representation, and perceived ineffectiveness. The combined population of the G7 countries is approximately 780 million, making up nearly 10% of the world's population. Together, they account for around 50% of global nominal net wealth and about 30% of the world's GDP as of 2024. In recent years, there has been ongoing debate about expanding the G7. Some, like Ronald A. Klain from the Carnegie Endowment, argue that due to the Eurocentric nature of the group and Japan being the sole Asian representative, it would make sense to include countries like South Korea and Australia, transforming the G7 into a G9.
